--- Q&A ITEM ---
question: My central AC runs constantly but the house won't cool below 78°F — the air from the vents feels only slightly cool. What should I check first?

answer: |
  Tools needed: replacement air filter (match the size printed on the old filter's frame),
  flashlight, indoor thermometer, garden hose, screwdriver (if opening air handler panel).

  1. Check and replace the air filter first. Locate the filter at the return-air grille
     (large louvered vent, usually in a hallway ceiling) or at the air handler unit itself.
     Slide out the old filter and hold it up to light — if you cannot see light through it,
     it is clogged. Insert the new filter with the arrow on the frame pointing toward the
     air handler (direction of airflow). A clogged filter causes roughly half of all
     "runs but won't cool" service calls and takes under two minutes to fix.
  2. Check the thermostat. Confirm it is set to COOL, the setpoint is below current room
     temperature, and the fan is set to AUTO (not ON). When ON is selected, the fan runs
     continuously and circulates uncooled air between compressor cycles, making supply air
     feel warm even when the system is working correctly.
  3. Inspect the outdoor condenser unit. Confirm the large fan on top is spinning during
     operation. Clear leaves, grass clippings, or debris from the sides and top. Use a
     garden hose with a gentle spray to rinse dirt off the fins from the inside outward
     (push debris out, not deeper in). Fins are fragile aluminum — do not use high pressure.
  4. Check for a frozen evaporator coil. Turn the system off at the thermostat but leave
     the fan set to ON. Locate the indoor air handler and feel the large insulated copper
     line entering it — if it is coated in ice, the coil is frozen. Let the fan run for
     one to two hours to thaw completely before switching back to COOL mode. A frozen coil
     is usually caused by a clogged filter or low refrigerant. If freezing recurs after
     replacing the filter, stop and call a licensed HVAC technician — refrigerant work
     requires EPA Section 608 certification and must not be attempted by homeowners.
  5. After completing steps 1–3, run the system for 15 minutes, then measure the
     supply-air temperature at a vent. It should read 15–20°F below current room
     temperature. If the delta is smaller than 15°F, the system needs professional service.

equipment_problem: Central AC runs constantly but house stays warm; vent air feels only slightly cool

tools_required:
  - replacement air filter (match size on old filter frame)
  - flashlight
  - indoor thermometer
  - garden hose (for condenser fin cleaning)
  - screwdriver (flathead or Phillips, for air handler panel if needed)

steps:
  - Remove and inspect old air filter; replace if clogged (hold up to light to test)
  - Confirm thermostat is set to COOL, setpoint below room temp, fan set to AUTO
  - Inspect outdoor condenser — confirm fan spins and clear debris from fins; rinse fins gently
  - Check indoor refrigerant line for ice; if frozen, run fan-only for 1–2 hours then restart
  - After repairs, measure vent supply-air temp — should be 15–20°F below room temperature
  - Call a licensed HVAC technician if cooling deficit persists (refrigerant requires certification)

safety_info: |
  Before opening the air handler cabinet or touching any internal components, turn the
  system completely off at the thermostat AND flip the dedicated AC circuit breaker to OFF
  at the panel — the air handler contains 240V capacitors that can hold a lethal charge even
  after the thermostat is turned off. Never attempt to add or recharge refrigerant: R-410A
  operates at pressures above 600 psi and requires EPA Section 608 certification to handle
  legally and safely.

tips:
  - Replace the air filter every 30–90 days depending on household occupancy and pets — set
    a phone calendar reminder at the same time each quarter so a clogged filter never causes
    a no-cool call in the middle of summer.
  - When rinsing condenser fins, always spray from inside the unit outward through the fin
    coil so debris is pushed out rather than compacted deeper into the coil.
  - A $10 indoor/outdoor thermometer with a probe lets you measure supply-air temperature at
    the vent in real time; the 15–20°F delta is a reliable DIY field check that tells you
    whether a technician visit is actually needed before you book one.
