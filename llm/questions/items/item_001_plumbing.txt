--- Q&A ITEM ---
question: My bathroom faucet drips constantly even when fully closed — how do I fix it?

answer: |
  Tools needed: adjustable wrench, flathead and Phillips screwdrivers, replacement washers
  and O-rings (bring the old ones to the hardware store to match size), plumber's grease.

  1. Turn off the water supply valves under the sink — turn clockwise until they stop.
     Verify water is off by opening the faucet fully until flow stops completely.
  2. Remove the decorative cap on top of the handle (pry gently with a flathead) and
     unscrew the handle screw underneath. Pull the handle straight up and off.
  3. Use the adjustable wrench to unscrew the packing nut — turn counterclockwise.
     Lift out the stem assembly straight up.
  4. Inspect the rubber seat washer at the bottom of the stem. If it is flattened,
     cracked, or has a groove worn into it, that is your leak source. Unscrew and
     replace it with an identical new washer.
  5. Also check and replace the O-rings on the stem body if they appear dry or cracked.
     Apply a thin coat of plumber's grease to all new rubber parts before reassembly.
  6. Reassemble in reverse order, hand-tighten the packing nut then add a quarter turn
     with the wrench. Restore water supply and test — the drip should be gone.

equipment_problem: Bathroom faucet drips constantly when fully closed

tools_required:
  - adjustable wrench
  - flathead screwdriver
  - Phillips screwdriver
  - replacement washers and O-rings
  - plumber's grease

steps:
  - Turn off hot and cold supply valves under the sink; confirm no flow at the open faucet
  - Remove decorative handle cap, unscrew handle screw, pull handle off
  - Unscrew packing nut counterclockwise with adjustable wrench; lift stem straight out
  - Inspect and replace the rubber seat washer at the bottom of the stem
  - Inspect and replace O-rings on the stem; apply plumber's grease to all new rubber parts
  - Reassemble in reverse; hand-tighten packing nut plus a quarter turn; restore supply and test

safety_info: |
  Turn off both supply valves (hot and cold) completely before disassembling. Confirm no
  water pressure remains by opening the faucet until flow stops — residual pressure can
  spray water forcefully when the packing nut is removed.

tips:
  - Match the replacement washer by taking the old one to the hardware store — washer
    diameters vary by 1–2 mm and even a slight mismatch will cause the new washer to leak
    or wear prematurely.
  - If the faucet still drips after replacing the washer, the brass seat inside the faucet
    body may be scored. A seat wrench ($8 tool) lets you unscrew and replace it without
    buying a whole new faucet.
